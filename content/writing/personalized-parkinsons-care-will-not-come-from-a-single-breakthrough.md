In 2023, a large multinational study reported that alpha-synuclein seed amplification assays could detect Parkinson's disease pathology with sensitivity and specificity that outperformed clinical diagnosis in several cohorts. The finding generated substantial enthusiasm. Here, finally, was a biological signal that could anchor diagnosis, stratify populations, and perhaps define who should receive a neuroprotective therapy once one becomes available.

The field is right to be excited. It should also be careful about what it concludes.

Alpha-synuclein pathology is present across a wide spectrum of disease severity and clinical presentation. A positive assay identifies biological involvement but does not specify trajectory, does not resolve phenotypic heterogeneity, and does not tell us which patient will progress rapidly, develop dementia, fall, or respond to a given treatment. It is a powerful signal. It is one signal.

This is the central tension in personalized Parkinson's care. The tools to characterize individual patients are improving across multiple domains simultaneously. The challenge is that no single tool, however refined, captures enough of the disease to support individualized decisions. Personalized care will emerge from the integration of multiple imperfect signals, each contributing partial information, combined in ways that reflect the complexity of the disease itself.

## The Data Silo Problem

Parkinson's disease generates an extraordinary volume of data. Large longitudinal cohorts have followed thousands of patients for years, collecting clinical assessments, biological specimens, and imaging. Digital health technologies have introduced continuous motor monitoring through wearable sensors. Electronic health records contain millions of clinical encounters with narrative documentation of symptoms, progression, and treatment response. Genetic studies have mapped an expanding landscape of risk variants.

Each of these data streams operates, in practice, largely independently.

Cohort studies produce structured datasets with carefully defined variables, though their populations are selected, their follow-up intervals are fixed, and their endpoints are often defined by rating scales designed decades ago. Digital sensors generate high-frequency objective data, though their clinical interpretation remains debated and the relationship between sensor-derived metrics and patient-relevant outcomes is still being established. Electronic health records capture the full diversity of real-world practice, though their data is unstructured, noisy, and shaped by documentation habits as much as by biology. Genetic data provides biological specificity, though for most patients it explains only a fraction of phenotypic variation.

The result is a field rich in data and fragmented in how it uses that data. The cohort researchers, the digital health engineers, the bioinformatics groups, and the geneticists often work in parallel rather than in concert. Models built within any single data stream inherit the limitations of that stream.

The integration challenge is both technical and conceptual. It requires defining what "personalized" means in a disease where clinical trajectories are heterogeneous, diagnostic labels can shift over time, and the outcomes that matter most to patients (mobility, falls, autonomy, cognitive function) are influenced by factors that no single measurement captures fully.

## What We Can Do Today

The present state of the field is more capable than the pessimistic narrative suggests.

We already have large, well-characterized cohorts with longitudinal follow-up. We already have seed amplification assays that can detect alpha-synuclein pathology in cerebrospinal fluid and, increasingly, in peripheral tissues. We already have dopamine transporter imaging with established clinical utility in specific diagnostic contexts. We already have genetic testing panels that identify actionable subgroups. Digital health technologies have generated promising measures of gait, tremor, and bradykinesia, though the validation work required to deploy them as clinical or trial endpoints is still actively underway.

What we lack is a systematic framework for combining these signals into models that inform individual-level decisions.

The building blocks exist. Multivariable prediction models using clinical and demographic features have been applied to progression forecasting in several cohorts. Machine learning approaches have been used to identify motor subtypes, predict medication response, and stratify fall risk. These models are imperfect — they often lack external validation, their generalizability across populations is uncertain, and their clinical deployment remains rare. They demonstrate, however, that integration is feasible with the data we have today.

The gap is in the architecture that connects data streams to clinical decisions.

## What Is Emerging

Several developments are converging in ways that may reshape this landscape over the next few years, and they are worth examining not just individually but in relation to each other.

Multimodal prediction models are beginning to combine clinical features, biomarkers, imaging, and in some cases genetic information within single frameworks. Rather than asking whether each data type predicts an outcome in isolation, these models ask how data types interact and whether their combination improves prediction beyond what any single source achieves alone. Early results are promising, though the field is still demonstrating feasibility rather than clinical utility. The harder question, which the emerging literature has not yet answered, is whether better multimodal prediction translates into better decisions — and for which patients.

Alongside this, the scale of available data is expanding in a direction that deserves particular attention. Electronic health records represent an underutilized resource for Parkinson's research. Clinical notes contain detailed descriptions of symptoms, functional status, medication effects, and adverse events that are often richer than what structured rating scales capture. Natural language processing and large language models are making it possible to extract and structure this information at scale, potentially expanding the dataset from thousands of research participants to millions of clinical encounters. The methodological challenges are real, but so is the potential to study outcomes that have historically been invisible to research.

The biological classification of the disease is also evolving in ways that create new opportunities for stratification. The growing understanding of genetic subtypes and the deployment of alpha-synuclein seed amplification assays are creating conditions for biologically anchored patient grouping. This does not mean the disease divides neatly into categories. For some patients, however, biological markers can now inform prognosis and treatment decisions in ways that clinical assessment alone cannot — and the integration of biological stratification with clinical and digital phenotyping is where some of the most consequential work is taking shape.

Finally, trial enrichment is gaining traction as a practical application of these developments. Selecting participants based on predicted progression rates, biological subtype, or multimodal risk profiles could improve statistical power and increase the probability of detecting treatment effects in populations most likely to benefit. If neuroprotective therapies are to succeed, the populations enrolled in trials may need to be more precisely defined than current eligibility criteria allow.

## What Remains Uncertain

Some aspects of personalized Parkinson's care remain genuinely speculative, and precision about this matters.

The clearest example is adaptive decision support. The vision of a system that continuously integrates new data from wearables, clinical visits, and laboratory results to update individualized recommendations in real time is conceptually compelling. The infrastructure for this does not exist in most health systems, the regulatory frameworks are underdeveloped, and the clinical validation required to trust such a system with consequential decisions has not been done. This is worth building toward. It is not the present.

There is also a deeper uncertainty that the field tends to understate. Integration improves prediction of who is at risk. It does not, in its current form, reliably explain why a given patient is at risk — and in a disease with multiple contributing mechanisms for each major outcome, the why is often the load-bearing clinical question. A patient at high risk for falls may be in that category because of freezing of gait, or orthostatic hypotension, or cognitive impairment, or medication timing. Each points to a different intervention. Risk stratification does not resolve this. Mechanistic attribution requires something more.

Acknowledging this gap is not a counsel of pessimism. It is a call for the field to be precise about what integration can deliver now, what will require additional methodological work, and what depends on developments that do not yet exist.

## The Integration Thesis

Personalized care in Parkinson's disease will emerge gradually, as the field learns to combine clinical observation, digital measurement, biological markers, and real-world data into models that inform and strengthen clinical judgment.

This requires moving beyond data silos. It requires building systems that can ingest heterogeneous inputs, accommodate missing data, and handle the temporal complexity of a progressive disease. It requires, perhaps most importantly, honesty about time horizons: some capabilities exist today and are underutilized, some are actively being developed, and some remain speculative.

The temptation to compress those time horizons is understandable. The field is advancing, funding is available, and there is genuine appetite for a coherent vision. But the clinicians who will ultimately decide whether to adopt these tools, and the patients whose care depends on that adoption, are best served by a version of the vision that is demanding about the evidence.

In late May, I will be exploring these themes at the [7th World Parkinson Congress](https://wpc2026.org/) in Phoenix, in a session on artificial intelligence, big data, and digital devices in Parkinson's care. The path forward runs through building connective tissue between the data we already have and the decisions that matter to patients — and being clear-eyed about how much of that work remains.
