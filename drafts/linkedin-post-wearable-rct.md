A well-validated wearable. 35 centers. 156 patients. 26 weeks.

Rodríguez-Molinero and colleagues cluster-randomized neurologists into three approaches for guiding PD medication adjustments: a standard clinical visit, the Hauser diary, or the STAT-ON Parkinson's Holter.

The STAT-ON is a waist-worn device that records motor state continuously and generates a PDF report with graphical summaries of time in ON, OFF, and intermediate states, freezing of gait episodes, and dyskinesia periods. The clinician reviews it at the next visit.

All three arms improved similarly at 26 weeks. OFF time, ON time, dyskinesia, PDQ-8 — equivalent across groups.

Here is what I think the result is actually telling us.

The 35 centers in this trial were academic and specialist neurology clinics. These are neurologists who already do this well. When the baseline quality of care is high, a new tool has limited room to move the needle further. That is a ceiling effect, and it is a reasonable explanation for what happened here.

There is a second factor. All three groups were being formally observed. Patients likely filled diaries more carefully, wore devices more consistently, and engaged more actively with their motor state than they would outside a trial. The Hawthorne effect likely elevated all three arms simultaneously, which narrows the differences between them.

This does not mean continuous motor monitoring is the wrong idea. It may mean we have been testing it in the wrong settings.

A general neurologist managing five Parkinson's patients in a busy mixed practice. A rural clinic with twice-yearly specialist access. A telehealth encounter where clinical observation is limited to a screen. In these contexts, four weeks of structured motor state data provides information the clinical encounter alone cannot generate. The information gap is wider. The tool has more room to work.

The evidence base needs trials in those settings — not just in the expert centers most likely to show a ceiling.
