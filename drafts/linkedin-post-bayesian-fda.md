The FDA just issued draft guidance endorsing Bayesian methods for clinical trials. Most coverage treats it as a general statistics story.

For Parkinson's disease, it's more specific than that — and I think it matters for how we design the next generation of neuroprotection trials.

Three things in the actual guidance that caught my attention:

**1. Skeptical priors are formally endorsed — and PD fits the stated rationale exactly.**
The document says skeptical priors are appropriate when "there have been a number of failed trials for closely related drugs in a therapeutic area." That is the history of PD neuroprotection. CoQ10, creatine, GDNF — and LEAP showed in 2019 that even levodopa doesn't modify disease, just treat symptoms. A formal skeptical prior means the data has to overcome that track record, not just beat a null.

**2. Natural history data can augment control arms.**
Section III.B of the guidance covers using external data to supplement randomized controls. PPMI — with its decade of longitudinal MDS-UPDRS, biomarkers, and genetic stratification — is the obvious candidate for exactly this. Smaller randomized control arms, same evidentiary standard.

**3. Borrowing across GBA-PD, LRRK2-PD, and idiopathic disease is explicitly in scope.**
Section III.D covers Bayesian hierarchical models across disease subtypes. Each subgroup doesn't need to be independently powered. The model borrows adaptively.

I wrote a commentary on this with a PD framing, plus an interactive simulation of both a symptomatic efficacy trial and a delayed-start disease modification trial — so you can see how the prior actually shifts the posterior under different assumptions.
